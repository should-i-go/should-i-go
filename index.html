Should I Go?


Aggregating special event data to avoid black swans in traffic

Problem & Impact

Problem Statement

In the world’s busiest cities Urban Traffic Jams are a regular nuisance. The price we pay for working and living at the center of it all. Most city dwellers learn the back roads and steer clear of rush hour to avoid typical congestion, but special events make the problem even worse. A parade, marathon, or sporting event can paralyze entire neighborhoods, and it’s keep track of when each event will occur.
These special events act as negative shocks to the traffic system, disrupting the regular cycle and typical travel times. In finance, they might be referred to as “black swans”: rare events that came as a surprise and had a major effect.

>

Why Chicago ?

Chicago is home to five of the top 20 worst roads for traffic congestion in the U.S
Northeastern Illinois-Northwest Indiana region suffered the misery of 61 extra hours behind the wheel on average in 2014 — equivalent to a week and a half of work — because of delays caused by gridlock, construction zones and collisions that tied up traffic

Why Chicago (cont.) ?

Chicago travel times during peak congestion periods are 131% of estimated travel time in free-flow periods.
At the 95th percentile, that ratio reached 316% in 2014.
In 2014, 35% of Chicagoans travel occurred during peak congestion periods.

Impact

If we improved Chicagoan’s exposure to gridlock by even 0.1%, we could reduce transportation costs by over $7 million dollars.
Improving Time in Traffic by just 1% saves over 4 person lifetimes/year in Chicago Alone
On a personal level, the rare events can result in a missed interview, expensive ticket gone waste, reschedule of medical emergency etc.

Our Solution



We propose a service that enhances traffic predictions by aggregating special event information and incorporating “black swan” events into forecasts.

We studied historical data to understand the incremental impact of special events - large sporting events and festivals - on Chicago traffic patterns. Leveraging that knowledge, along with online sources of upcoming events, we can improve traffic forecasts by adding a new variable to the model: special events.

This additional dimension of information allows Chicago residents and visitors to make better informed choices when planning events. Not just “What is the typical commute time for that neighborhood?” but “Given upcoming sport and entertainment events, what will traffic congestion look like next Wednesday?”

Data Science

Data

Data Collection: Chicago Traffic

The Chicago Traffic Tracker estimates traffic congestion on 300 miles of principal arterial streets in real-time by continuously monitoring and analyzing GPS traces received from Chicago Transit Authority buses. Congestion estimates provide the observed speed, typically for one-half mile of a street in one direction of traffic, and are produced every 10 minutes.

Data Collection: Special Event Permits

The City of Chicago provides open access to its database of event permits. We were particularly interested in the “Festival/Performance/12,001+ Event” permit, granted for events on City of Chicago property anticipating more than 12,000 attendees. Examples include Lollapalooza and the Chicago marathon. The database spans 2012 through November 2016 - including both historical and upcoming events.

Data Collection: Sporting Events

Basketball-reference.com and Pro-football-reference.com provided historical game data sets for the Chicago Bulls and Bears. This included game dates, start times, home or away, and the number of overtimes.

Data Processing: Python

To combine the data sets, we used iPython Notebook and Pandas, an open source library which provides data structure and analysis tools for Python. We imported historical traffic, sporting event and festival events data. The next step translating disparate system timestamps into a uniform format, eliminating unnecessary fields, and calculating each segment’s central geographical coordinates. To process sporting data, we used the type of game, start times and the number of overtimes to approximate game end timestamps. We used trigonometry to calculate the distance between traffic segment coordinates and game locations.

Challenges and Data Cleaning

We encountered several data issues with the Chicago Traffic Tracker estimates. For many records, segment data was recorded at ten minute intervals as advertised, but every timestamp had been hard-coded to 11:50PM. This meant that, although we had 144 records for each day, we could not ascertain the correct order of the time series data. We could only study the general distribution of traffic speeds on each day.
A more entertaining anomaly was city bus bedtime. We noticed that there were very few, and odd data points between 11pm and 4am. This makes sense when you consider that all busses were in the yard and garage, only being moved for maintenance. We needed to discard those data points.

Data Analysis

The limitations of our time series meant that analysis was not as straight-forward as we hoped. In the end, the team used regional, every-ten-minute data to understand daily rush hour cycles and segment-level, daily data to understand the impact of special events on surrounding arterial streets. If we continue this project in the future, we will collect and store real-time Chicago Traffic Tracker data over a period of 6-12 months, with the correct time stamps, to build a more comprehensive forecasting foundation.

Results

Our analysis indicates that, on special event days, affected traffic segments’ average speed can be reduced by X-Y miles per hour. A traffic forecast based on typical traffic patterns would indicate expected speeds of J-K miles per hour, but a forecast including the special event forecasts speeds reduced to M-N miles per hour due to congestion.

This is the value of our project: leveraging a new variable - special events - to improve the accuracy of travel forecasts, allow millions of commuters to navigate their cities more efficiently, and reduce the compounding effects of traffic congestion on city streets.
