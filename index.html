The 2015 Nepal Earthquake

Stories, Impact, and Next Steps

An Earthquake Unfolding

Read on below, and use the interactive map (add layers at top right) to understand more how the earthquake unfolded .

Nepal's population

Nepal has a population of 27.8m as of 2013. However, due to its mountainous terrain much of the population lives along its borders with India where the land is flatter. Notice the darker blue areas along the borders of the interative map to the right. The exception is Kathmandu with a population of over 1m living, or rather, perched in the Kathmandu valley at an elevation of 1400m.

An epicenter close to the capital

The epicenter of the Nepal earthquake occured only 80km NorthWest of Kathmandu, registering at 7.8 on the richter scale. This meant that, while the full force was not felt, the earthquake still shifted the entire city 3 meters south! Add the "Earthquake Magnitude" layer to the map on the right to see where the eartquake hit.

Towns flattened, buildings destroyed

The result was the destruction of many towns and buildings across Nepal; particularly those close to the epicenter. 9,000 people were killed and 23,000 injured by the initial shock, with a further 200 killed as a result of aftershocks. The worst loss of life, though, was not felt in the capital. Add the # buildings damaged and # deaths layers to visualise the impact.

The story of Sindhupalchowk

The largest number of deaths occured in the Sindhupalchowk region of Nepal. This area, typically easily accessible, became treacherous to reach post-earthquake due to its steep mountains, narrow roads and the large number of landslides that occured. It took the first rescue vehicles more than 3 hours to arrive from Kathmandu.

Locations of landslides

Looking at the location of landslides subsequent to the earthquake it is clear to see that these occured in the regions North of Kathmandu, stretching into Sindhupalchowk.

How the World Responded

After the earthquake, governments, NGOs, and communities provided quick support. While millions have been raised to date and disbursed in several forms, roughly half the pledged funds are still pending distribution. This is, in part, due to the long rebuilding process that still remains ahead.

Design Philosophy and Goals

Our goal for this project is to create a "data journalism" piece to inform, engage, and educate users about the 2015 Nepal Earthquake and the associated (and still ongoing) rebuilding process. Our intended audience isn't necessarily data scientists or international development experts, instead we aim to reach the members of the general public who may be broadly familiar with the disaster in Nepal but unaware of most of the details.

For this reason, we've chosen a mix of interactive visualizations of quantitative data and first-person narrative accounts and photos. This approach enables us to depict broad trends, but without sacrificing the personal stories from the affected communities.


Tools Used

  • Most visualizations on this page were created with D3. We chose D3 as our primary visualization tool because of its powerfand versatility, as well as the opportunity it presented to learn a new javascript library.
  • The interactive map describing providing a summary of the Earthquake is powered by CartoDB, which is great for maps that might be too onerous to set up in D3
  • We use the Twitter Bootstrap framework for the rest of the site structure since it's well-documented and makes building responsive websites simple. Page layout is based on free/open source Just One Page Bootstrap theme
  • All code for this project is stored and versioned in GitHub
  • This page is hosted cheaply and easily via Amazon Web Services


User Testing

To ensure we're offering the most effective and engaging visualizations we can, we reached out to friends and family to offer their feedback via a structured user testing framework. Participants were given a list of tasks to complete on the page and asked to articulate their thought process outloud. For example, we asked users questions like "Where was the epicenter of the earthquake and what was its magnitude on the Richter scale?" and "Why were deaths so severe in the Sindhupalchok region?". After working through the examples, users were also invited to provide open-ended feedback. Based on the responses from our testers, we focused on clarifying the narrative, eliminating unnecessary text and non-data ink, and making the visualizations easier to use.
